Chapter 1

Dawn of Consciousness

Morning broke slow over Otautau, though "broke" was too lively a word for the sluggish pall that clung to the township that day. The twenty-fourth of November, nineteen seventy-two, ought to have been an ordinary morning like any other, stitched together by the gentle hum of rural routine

Normality

The milkman rattled his bottles down gravel drives, each clink echoing sharper than it should. In kitchens across the town, transistor radios murmured stock prices and sheep tallies, voices flattened by static as though broadcast from some immeasurable distance.

And Then It Echoed

An intolerable Cry. Not the squall of an infant, nor the anguish of any creature known to these paddocks and pastures. It rose like a wound torn in the fabric of the morning, raw, ancient, and unplaceable. Birds scattered, their chorus cut mid-note. Dogs barked and then fell silent, whining low as though cowed by some presence unseen. Even the milkman swore his bottles rang with a hollow, unnatural pitch. In the small local weatherboard maternity hospital, curtains drawn tight against the grey, a child had been born.

Freed From The Womb

I was free from mother's womb. I came into this world with hair sable as the abyss betwixt the stars, and eyes so fathomless in their umber that they seemed to drink the very light from the chamber. The midwife, a woman whose hands had ushered countless infants into breath, faltered.Her eyes widened, her lips parted, yet no prayer nor comfort crossed them. For though swaddled and mute, my gaze was ancient, terrible, knowing a stare that seemed to carry memories ancient and secret

Embracing the Unknown

My mother reclined in her white-linen bed, her skin pale with the exhaustion of labour and blood loss, yet her lips curved in a weary smile. She knew not—how could she?—that her agony was but the prelude to a torment yet unseen, coiled like a serpent in the folds of time, waiting its hour to strike.Outside, the town stirred uneasily.
In the quiet of her room, my mother slept, her smile faint, her breathing shallow. I lay swaddled at her side, eyes wide open, unblinking, drinking in the dim light of the world that had birthed me.In the heart of Otautau, a shadow lengthened. Not of cloud nor hill, nor any earthly obstruction, but of the inexorable hand of Fate itself. Thus, the peace was shattered.

Chapter 2

The Unfolding

House Of Unrest

The years that followed my birth did not unfold with pastoral peace but with a grinding discord, as though time itself turned against me. The tranquillity that once veiled Otautau faded to memory, and within our small home I grew in unrest. Something within me rebellion, or perhaps illness coiled in my body like a viper, unseen yet unrelenting. The towns physicians, cold in their craft, could name no cause and offer no cure. My pain was a riddle, my suffering a ghost.

Body And Mind

It was not the body alone that betrayed me, but the mind. Thoughts clashed and roared like a storm, a thousand voices rising and falling within. I could not silence them nor escape. The world of laughter and games beyond the window belonged to others. For me, every sound was an intrusion, every moment a battle against the noise within my own skull.

See Me

In preschool I moved like a ghost among children. While they built towers and sang songs, I sat apart, staring into spaces they could not see. They sensed my strangeness and kept their distance, or else mocked what they did not understand. I longed not for attention but for recognition for someone to see me, truly see me. When none did, I turned to mischief, desperate to prove that I existed.

To Dread That Which You Love

My mother watched me fade behind eyes weary from sleepless years and unanswered prayers. She had conquered pain once to bring me into the world, yet now it returned not to her body, but her heart. Love warred with dread each time she looked at me. The gulf between us widened, silent and sorrowful, until even comfort became a stranger in our house.

Darkness Gathers

Thus passed my earliest years; too loud for a quiet world, too restless for peace. Beneath Otautaus calm face, something darker gathered. The town slept, but within me a storm began to stir a storm that even now, I fear, had only just awakened.

Chapter 3

Chalk Dust And Dreams

The Restless

I was no ordinary scholar barely a mediocre one. From the moment I entered that grim institution, I was an exile among its ranks, a spirit misaligned with the world it sought to shape. The classrooms chalk dust and endless rows felt more like a cell than a sanctuary. My first teacher, kind and patient, tried to reach me, but her words drowned in the storm within thoughts clashing like armies, fears nameless yet relentless. To sit still was triumph; to think clearly, a dream. My eyes wandered always to the window, searching for my fathers truck and the promise of escape, a world unburdened by expectation.

Cigarettes And The Nectar Of The Gods

Curiosity betrayed me one weekend when I stole my fathers Pall Mall cigarettes, believing he chewed them. Beneath my bed, I bit into one, swallowed, and was seized by violent sickness. His wrath came swiftly, but it was his silence that cut deepest. His solace was amber and bottled, and I feared I was its cause, the source of his sorrow, my mothers tears. At six, I longed to ride to the pub and bring him home, but her trembling hands held me back. Even then, I felt like both child and curse.

The Foor Is Burning

Fire became my tutor. First paper, then a bedspread, then, after Guy Fawkes, the hedge itself. The flames danced, the fire brigade roared, and the captains quiet rebuke to my father burned hotter than the blaze. Rage chased me down Merrivale Road, his fury the language I knew best. Then, when his back shattered, pills replaced the bottle, and silence swallowed our home. In my seventh year, a classmate and his mother died in a fire; I laughed nerves, not cruelty but they saw mockery. The torment that followed was its own slow burning inferno

Fighting Had Reward

“Turn the other cheek,” my mother said. “Fight back,” commanded my father. So I did. I hurled a bully through the headmasters glasshouse and bore the punishment alone. The deputys belt cracked my palms, yet I laughed pain mistaken for defiance. That laughter earned me three more lashes. When my father heard, he took me for a milkshake and gummies, his first and only tenderness, sweet as it was bewildering. But betrayal followed swiftly; my class denied its longed for camp to Deep Cove, my parents unmoved. I learned then that neglect can wound more sharply than any belt

Bitch

Anger turned to rebellion. I called my mother “bitch,” ducked her swing, and fled to the solitude I craved. At twelve, I walked fifteen kilometres into the night, a runaway under indifferent stars. The constable found me and returned me to silence and scorn. Pain became my inheritance both of body and soul. When illness at last unmanned me, the doctors uncovered two hernias and a trapped testicle. Months of half days followed, my legs bound like a marionettes. In the hospital I met Dickybird, another exile. We laughed, defied, and raised mischief in our plaster prisons. Yet beneath the laughter lingered truth; we were both broken things, stitched by surgeons, bound by pain, brothers not by blood but by survival.

Chapter 5

Visitation Of The Dark Kind

Escape And Disillusionment

The visions of war, of violence, of power unearned but seized, stalked me like phantoms in every shadow. I was no longer a student, but a boy adrift without map, without compass, without mercy. A tempest of flesh and bone, unseen by all, unheard by all, a storm unspent, gathering still. Freedom, I had believed, would be my balm, the draught to soothe my long suffering soul.
I thought that once unshackled from the jeers of tormentors, from the bus, from the flagpole of disgrace, I might begin anew. Beyond those gates, I imagined some sanctified realm where I could become normal, though even then, I scarce knew what such a word could mean. Yet fate cruel, mocking fate had conspired otherwise. It began insidiously, a fevers whisper behind the eyes, a spark that would soon devour me.

The Web Of Delerium

My limbs grew leaden, my skull heavy as stone. Then delirium seized me in its merciless grip. They named it viral meningitis, as though the syllables could rob it of dread. But I knew it for what it was: a descent. Hallucinations, hideous and profane, beset me. Faces dead, unknown, yet familiar crowded my sight. They spoke in voices like wind whistling through shards of glass.
And demons O dreadful demons! danced at the edges of vision, their eyes twin embers of baleful fire, gleaming with mirthless delight. The walls themselves seemed to breathe, swelling and contracting as though the house were a living beast that watched, mocked, and hungered. Day and night lost all meaning, melding into one fever-soaked phantasmagoria where waking and dreaming were but reflections in the same black mirror

The Veil Between Here And There

Weeks passed or centuries. My body withered beneath the fevers tyranny, yet my mind burned ever brighter, unhinged, unbound. When at last the fever relented, I found not healing, but transformation.The earth beneath my feet no longer held firm; it shifted like treacherous sands, for I had glimpsed beyond the veil.
And the veil, merciless and unblinking, had stared back.
Something vast and ancient had stirred within me cruel, patient, eternal. The hallucinations faded, but the sense of being watched remained.
The air itself seemed heavy with presence. Had I recovered, or had something taken root within my soul? It toyed with me, not with words, but with feelings; dread, rage, and a solitude so profound it seemed almost sacred. I wore a mask of normalcy laughed, jested, pretended but beneath it, the noise raged on, whispering; freak, outcast, curse incarnate.

Obsession

Violence became my dark obsession not for its gore, but for its cruel clarity. War, at least, made sense; there were enemies, weapons, and purpose. I dreamt myself soldier, mercenary, predator in a world that despised me.
I built traps, devised ambushes, studied Sun Tzu's tactics as written. My war was waged not in fields, but within the hollow of my skull. And when my father fell victim to one of my contrivances, guilt drove me to bury the darkness beneath routine. Yet the mind, once opened to the abyss, can never forget and the abyss once roused, never sleeps.
Then, upon a night steeped in silence, the world itself shifted.

On The Edge

Moonlight, pale and merciless, sliced through my room like a blade. A hooded figure stood there, suspended above the floor silent, unreal. Its message thrummed through my bones: ancient, terrible, inevitable. Then it drifted through the wall and vanished. I felt no fear and that absence was the truest horror. Some buried part of me had always known it would come. It was not a dream, but a visitation. In the days that followed, its presence clung to me; sleep became a battlefield of whispers;
You are marked. You are watched. You are not alone.
When I woke, trembling, blade in hand, the voice murmured within: You called it a dream. I call it an arrival. I have always been here waiting for you to open the door. And so I buried the words, though shallowly for the darkness now had a hood.
